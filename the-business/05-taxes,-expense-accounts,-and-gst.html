Taxes, Expense Accounts, and GST

  • If the reporter's relationship with the firm is that of a subcontractor, the reporter must pay their income tax quarterly to CRA
  • When reporters receive income from work, a percentage of that income needs to be set aside for tax purposes, including paying CPP (recommendation: 30%)
  • Taz laws change every year - to make sure reporters are taking advantage of all the tax deductions to them and to avoid the potential of a possible audit, they may want to seek professional help
  • Tax calculator: https://simpletax.ca/calculator?&gclid=EAIaIQobChMIxbObrYTA5wIVj8VkCh3wUgo5EAAYAyABEgLZ_fD_BwE
  • Tax deduction: reduces the amount of income that's subject to tax (e.g., operational court reporting expenses, RRSPs)
  • Tax credit: reduces the amount of tax owing
    • An amount eligible for a tax credit generally creates a federal tax savings based on the lowest tax bracket, 15%
    • A 10,000 tax credit amount will generate $1,500 actual tax credit against taxes owing, which means your tax bill is lower by $1,500 (10,000 x 15%)
    • The calculation is the same no matter the tax bracket (e.g., tuition expenses, donations, student loan interest, medical expenses, Canada child benefit, child care deduction, age)
  • Marginal tax rate: the amount of tax paid on any additional dollar made up to the next tax bracket
  • Average tax rate: percentage of tax paid based on your total taxable income and reflects the total tax you are paying
    Tax Deductions
  • the reporter doesn't have to claim the deduction in the year they purchased the item
  • some of the deductions reporters generally are allowed include:
    1. Anything purchased to help reporters perform their job, including shorthand machine, computer hardware and software. Some items are depreciated over time (shorthand machine) and some can be written off 100% at the time of purchase (software).
    2. Depending on the working arrangement with the firm, car expenses including gas and maintenance. A log needs to be kept of business versus personal travel including date, destination, purpose, and number of kilometres driven (see example).
    3. Full cost of some utility expenses, including home Internet and phone expenses if used for the job.
    4. Partial cost of other utility expenses, including power and heat. Based on a percentage of office space with the rest of the house (see example).
    5. Any items purchased to create a home office.
  • Note: Generally, items 3, 4, and 5 can only be claimed if an office is not provided for you at the place you work.
    How to Claim Care Expenses
  • Example
    • On January 1 the odometer on your vehicle says 10,000 kms.
    • On December 31 the odometer on your vehicle says 30,000 kms.
    • By keeping a log, you determine 5,000 kms. were business related.
    • From this example, you are able to write off 25% of your vehicle expenses. 5,000/20,000=25%
    • Note: There are different rules for a purchased vehicle.
  • Write-Offa Include:
    - Leasing
    - Gas
    - Servicing and Repairs
    - Insurance
    - Parts
    - Licence and Registration fees
    - Car Washes
    - Note: Parking is a 100% write-off if done for business purposes.
    How to Claim Home Expenses
  • Layout Particulars
    - Apartment is 1,000 square feet
    - Office is 100 square feet (10 x10)
    - 100/1,000 means you can write off 10% of the following items:
    - Rent (get advice)
    - Gas and electricity
    - Water
    - General home repairs not particular to a given room.
    - You can still write of 100% of the following items:
    - Internet
    - Cell phone
    - Items and repairs for your office only (some of these may be depreciated)

    Employment Insurance Benefits for the Self-Employed
  • Optional benefit a self-employed person can pay for through a tax deduction.
  • Benefit: Financial support of up to 55% of earnings to maximum of $650 per week (2023).
  • Types of benefits: maternity, parental, sickness, family caregiver for children, compassionate care.
  • Conditions:
    • Be registered in self-employed program for at least 12 months
    • Decreased the amount of time you spend on your business by more than 40% for at least one week.
    • Have earned a minimum amount of net self-employed earnings between January 1 and December 31 of the year before you apply for benefits.
  • How to register:
    • Register online for the program through My Service Canada Account (MSCA)
    • Pay EI premiums
  • You can apply for special benefits 12 months after your confirmed registration date.
  • Estimate of Premiums
    • For 2023, you will pay $1.63 in EI premiums for every $100 earned to a maximum of $1,002.45. (Rate changes annually)
    • Same rate as an employee would pay.
  • NOTE: “Once you receive benefits as a self-employed person, you’ll continue paying premiums for as long as you’re self-employed.”
    Expense Accounts
  • Arrangements are usually made for reporters to be reimbursed when they have to travel out of town. If the reporter is paying for the expenses out their own pocket (to be reimbursed later), it is best to pay with a credit card. If the reporter happens to lose their receipt, then they will still have a record of the transaction.
  • When reporters do out-of-town work and receive money for meals and/or mileage (e.g., $50 per day for meals and 50 cents a kilometer), they do not have to claim that money as income on their income tax returns.
  • If a reporter does receive money for mileage, they cannot claim that trip as a taxable vehicle expense on their income tax return.
    GST - Goods and Services Tax
  • When Must GST Registration Take Place?
    • You have 29 days to apply for GST registration from the day that your business exceeds the small supplier threshold amount.
    • If on June 1 your revenues go over $30,000, you have until June 29 to apply for registration.
    • Note: June 1 would be my effective GST registration date, and I will be responsible for collecting GST/HST from that date.
  • How to register for the GST
    • GST registration with the CRA is fairly easy. You can register by phone, online, by mail or drop into your local CRA office. What's the GST Registration Process provides the phone numbers and addresses you need.
    • When you've registered, you'll be assigned a business number. This is a business identification number that you'll use for all your dealings with the CRA.
