Role of the Court Reporter

Before Proceedings Begin

Arrival

  • Arrive 30 minutes before the proceeding is set to commence (45-60 minutes if it's videotaped or realtime)
  • Introduce yourself when you enter the law office
  • Set up at one end of the table in the discovery room
    • Preferably away from the door
    • Consider where the electrical outlets and coffee stations are located
  • Introduce yourself and hand out business cards to lawyers

Setup

  • Set up your equipment first
    • Make sure everything is working properly
  • Request a style of cause
    • The examining lawyer will have the style of cause
      • Ask for a copy of it or ask if you can take a picture of it
    • It must be written exactly the same way as it's given to you, including capitalization and/or spelling errors
    • If time permits, quickly scan the style of cause for spellings and to learn about the case you're about to write

Appearances

  • When working with unfamiliar counsel, ask for their name and who they represent

  • Examples:

    • Unfamiliar Counsel
      Court Reporter: Sir, may I have your name, please.
      Lawyer: John R. Jones
      Court Reporter: And whom do you represent?
      Lawyer: I am on for the plaintiff.

      Familiar Counsel
      Court Reporter: Mr. Jones, who are you representing today?
      Lawyer: I am on for the plaintiff, Martin Wilson.

  • Always ask for the witness's full name
    *Would you please state your name.
    Could you tell me your full name, please.

  • If the witness's name is on the style of cause, you may confirm the spelling.
    Is this the correct spelling of your name?

  • If the witness's name is not on the style of cause, ask them to spell their name.

  • If the witness's name is different on the style of cause, confirm the correct spelling.

Information Sheet

  • Keep track of names, spellings, and times of the examination in a notebook or an information sheet.
  • Some firms will provide you with a template. Billing from expected start time in brackets, not actual start time. Good place to track exhibit numbers.
    (10:00)
    10:10 -
    Martin John Wilson
    Examined by Counsel Jones

Setup

  • Make dictionary entries, speaker IDs, and get yourself ready to write.
  • Do whatever it takes to feel comfortable before the proceeding starts.

Administering the oath

  • Administer an oath to the witness prior to examination commencing.
  • Ask if the witness would like to swear on the Bible or take an affirmation. Counsel will help you to explain if the witness doesn't understand the difference (promise to tell the truth vs. swearing to tell the truth on a Bible)
  • Make eye contact with the witness while administering the oath.
  • State the oath in a firm and steady voice.
  • Oath with a Bible:
    Do you solemnly swear the evidence you are about the give will be the truth, the whole truth, and nothing but the truth so help you God?
  • Affirmation:
    Do you solemnly affirm the evidence you are about to give will be the truth, the whole truth, and nothing but the truth?
  • People with religious beliefs other than Christian may choose to make an affirmation
  • People with no religious beliefs may choose to make an affirmation
  • With an interpreter:
    First administer the oath to the interpreter:
    Do you solemnly swear to well and truly interpret from the English language to the language and from the language to the English language to the best of your skill and ability so help you God?
    Do you solemnly affirm to well and truly interpret from the English language to the language and from the language to the English language to the best of your skill and ability so help you God?
    Then administer the oath to the witness through the interpreter.

During Pretrial Proceedings

  • Main role during the proceeding is to write it verbatim, but other tasks include:
    • Dealing with discussions off the record
    • Reporter readbacks
    • Anticipating counsel interjections
    • Interrupting speakers
    • Marking exhibits
    • Obtaining proper spellings
    • Indicating non-verbal actions
    • Handling heated arguments, asides, and conversation by third parties
    • Handling adjournments

Marking Exhibits

  • At pretrial proceedings, exhibits are marked by the reporter by affixing an exhibit stamp.  The exhibit stamp should be red in color and should be filled out by the reporter with a blue pen. This is to clearly distinguish a document marked as an exhibit from any copies that may later be made.
    • You may use pre-made stickers instead of stamps if they fit on the document being marked
  • To facilitate quick reference by counsel, placement of the exhibit stamp should be the top right corner of any document. If the top right corner is not available, place the stamp as close to the top of the document as possible. If there is no place on the entire face of the document to put the exhibit stamp, place the stamp on the back of the document.
    • It is prudent practice to let counsel know that you are putting the exhibit stamp on the back of the document.
  • Make sure when you are placing the stamp, that the stamp does not touch any written portion of the document.
  • When marking photographs, have prepared exhibit stamp stickers to place on the back of the photograph. The ink smudges when you stamp the back of photographs (with the shiny finish).  Stickers are often used for documents as well.

The reporter would include the following information in the exhibit stamp:

  1. Sequential number (or letter if marked for identification)
  2. Full name of witness (same as given when sworn in, all caps)
  3. Date of examination
  4. Reporter’s name and initials

Discussions off the Record

  • The reporter goes off the record only when both sides agree to go off the record
    • If opposing counsel gives no response or any type of indication to the requesting counsel wanting to go off the record, the reporter can assume opposing counsel is fine with going off the record.
  • The reporter should make it obvious that he or she is not writing anything that is being said.  Make an attempt to be obvious that you are off the record.
  • When counsel make the request to go off the record, it is recorded in the transcript unless the lawyer talks directly to the reporter only to go off the record; however, when the request is made to go back on the record, that is not included in the transcript.
  • When counsel are discussing whether or not to go off the record, that is included in the transcript.

Interrupting Speakers

  • Unlike clarifying the spelling of a word, you must make the interruption immediately to clarify something not heard or understood. If you hesitate, the speakers move on, and it is difficult to capture the moment again.
  • Say something short and to the point (“pardon me”) while making eye contact with the speaker. 
  • If you are clarifying a word that you think is the response but are not satisfied, repeat the response to the speaker.  For example: Did you say “yes”? If you didn’t hear an answer, don’t expect your audio to pick up the sound.
  • If you are unsure whether you heard a name or word correctly and can put that word into context later, make a notation and clarify the word during a break. If it's a proper name, you will have to get the spelling anyway.
  • Witnesses with accents can be difficult to understand, so be sure to advise examining counsel you are experiencing this difficulty.  Lawyers can then change the way they ask questions and will understand if you are interrupting often.
  • To clarify the spelling of a word, make a notation in your transcript. "Oops" is a good Case CAT feature for this! You can then ask your questions at the next break. A good resource for the spelling of proper nouns is from the documents themselves. Otherwise, you will direct your question to the witness or counsel to get a spelling. Record the information directly into your transcript or on your information page. 
  • The internet is a great resource for researching names of geographic areas, company names, street names, etc. 
  • It should be the goal of the reporter to clarify any information needed before they leave the discovery room. It will save time when editing.
  • You can try to tell a witness to slow down, but it often doesn't work. Do not interrupt if the lawyer is going for the "kill," the witness is reacting to the questions and not thinking, don't spoil the lawyer's train of thought or give the witness time to think.

Interjections

  • One of the biggest adjustments NAIT graduates have when they start out in industry is writing interjections by the witness's lawyer. One way to best deal with this scenario is to anticipate when an interjection or objection will be made.

Here is a list of possible reasons for an interjection or objection from opposing counsel:

  • Hypothetical questions. Examining counsel are not allowed to ask hypothetical questions, e.g., "If you weren't speeding, do you think the accident would have happened?"
  • Questions that ask for an opinion. Examining counsel are not allowed to ask for an opinion from the witness unless the witness is called as an expert in a specific field, e.g., "Do you think Mr. Jones did a good job in his position as manager?" gray area when it's about the witness's frame of mind
  • Questions that do not relate to the pleadings in a questioning for discovery or questions that do not fall within the “four corners” of an affidavit in a cross-examination on affidavit, e.g., if we had a car accident, and the witness was not claiming future loss of income because they got over their injuries after a certain amount of time, examining counsel cannot ask questions about their future employment and potential income
  • When examining counsel asks for an undertaking - lawyer will usually step in and answer for the witness
  • When the questioning becomes heated between the counsel and the witness, the witness’s lawyer may interject to protect his or her witness, i.e., they will make an objection just to stop the proceeding and give their witness's a chance to compose themselves again and think about their answers
  • The body language of the witness’s lawyer. Many times the witness’s lawyer will put his hand in front of witness or move up to the end of his chair so the witness knows not to answer the question and the lawyer can interject.

Note: In Canadian proceedings, witnesses do not answer questions that are objected to. You may see the witness answering in the demo videos from NCRA Career Launcher.

Readbacks

  • Reporter readbacks must be performed proficiently upon request. Many times the competency of the reporter is judged by their ability to read back.
  • When asked to read back, take your time. Review your text or vertical notes prior to beginning the readback.
  • The readback should be in a loud, clear voice at a steady pace (approximately 140 wpm). You must give the speaker identification first, e.g., "Question: Where were you on the night in question? Answer: I was - Speaker: Ms. Smith: Don't answer that."
  • Readbacks should be read with back-up equipment on. If the readback is not the last question or answer, highlight or mark the spot that was read back, and copy and paste in the appropriate spot.
  • Sometimes the readback requested is not only the last question asked or answer given but is not specific. If necessary, ask clarifying questions to determine what is being requested to be read.
  • If you need a few minutes to find the portion to read back or would like to review it with the audio backup, ask for a brief adjournment.

Obtaining Spellings

  • To clarify the spelling of a word, make a notation in your steno notes or make a note on your information page. You can then get the spelling at the next break.
  • The best place to get spellings is from the documents themselves. Otherwise, depending on the spelling needed, you will have to ask the witness or possibly counsel to get a spelling. Once you receive the spelling, record the spelling directly into your transcript and/or in the top right-hand corner on the back of your information page. Some spellings, like geography and company names, can also be attained from the Internet.
  • In terms of spellings, it should be the goal of the reporter to attain all necessary spellings before he/she leaves the questioning room. As the reporter, you don't want to waste time when producing the transcript having to look things up.

Indicating Non-Verbal Actions

  • When a witness nods his head to indicate “yes” or shakes his head to indicate “no”, counsel may ask for a verbal response.  If they do not, you may indicate to counsel there was no verbal response.
  • If a witness uses a body motion to answer a question, counsel may describe the motion on the record or ask the witness to describe it.
    Q Where did you hit your head?  (Witness points to his head)   (New Paragraph)
    I notice you are indicating the left temple area; is that correct?
    A Correct. 
  • If the lawyer doesn't do this, the reporter should interrupt and indicate there was no verbal response on the record.
  • We do not describe non-verbal actions in the record. There's no way to verify and it can be left to interpretation.

Heated Arguments

  • If there is a heated argument between counsel and the witness, do not interrupt and do the best you can.
  • If the heated argument is between counsel and their speech is overlapping, immediately step in and remind them to speak one at a time.

Conversation by Third Parties

  • If an observer/layperson speaks during a questioning and is not at the time under oath, immediately interject and ask whether the comments are to be included in the record.
  • If the comments are included in the record, the speaker is identified in colloquy format, e.g., MR. BROWN:

Handling Adjournments

  • Similar to discussions off the record, all counsel must agree to adjourn.
  • Like discussions off the record, the reporter quits writing when both sides agree to adjourn.

Apply Professional Conduct During Proceedings

  • You should provide professional conduct from the time you leave your home until you get back. How you act and present yourself during discussions off the record, adjournments, and in setting up and dismantling your equipment is just as important as when you write the proceeding.

After Pretrial Proceedings

  • Ask counsel if they would like to order a transcript
  • Check for delivery deadlines
    • Regular (5-10 business days)
    • Expedited (3-5 business days)
    • Next day
  • Counsel may sometimes put a transcript order on hold. This means they don't want it produced right away but may order a copy in the future.
    • Questionings in Aid
  • Upload backup digital files to your computer
  • Have all files named and properly organized
  • Obtain spellings from counsel or witness that you likely won't be able to find yourself
  • Be personable and polite

Prepare the Transcript

  • Write
    • Goal is to get everything down and as accurate as possible. In essence, write like you have no back-up audio.
    • Ask counsel or the witness for spellings you won't be able to easily find.
  • Edit with audio
    • Edit the transcript applying the rules of proper grammar and punctuation
    • Mark spots where further research may be needed
  • Proofread with audio
    • Proofread carefully with the audio
  • Proofread without audio
    • Read for context
    • You don't know what you don't know! Check everything!

Proofreading Tips for New Reporters

  1. New reporters may find proofreading on paper is more effective
  2. Always proofread for context first; ensure the punctuation portrays the correct meaning
  3. Read one word at a time; spelling errors and misused words are common errors
  4. Check the page for proper formatting and speaker tokens
  5. If your mentor is proofreading your transcripts, keep a log of common errors. You don't want to submit transcripts the with same errors
