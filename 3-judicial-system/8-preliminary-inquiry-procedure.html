Preliminary Inquiry Procedure

Preliminary Inquiries

  • Pretrial hearings held in Provincial Court
  • For offences proceeding by Indictment where the max penalty is 14 years or more
  • To determine if there is sufficient evidence for the case to proceed to trial in the Court of King's Bench
  • The accused may waive the preliminary inquiry with consent of the Crown
    • The accused will be ordered to stand trial without the need for further evidence
  • Direct indictment: allows Crown to bypass preliminary inquiry and proceed straight to trial
    • Only in rare and exceptional circumstances because it takes away the accused's right to a preliminary inquiry
      • Extremely long/complex cases
      • To protect ongoing police investigations
      • May cause significant harm to a witness (psychological or physical)
      • A preliminary was commenced but not concluded
      • There is deliberate delay of proceedings
    • Requires written consent of the Attorney General

R. v. Stinchcombe [1991] 3 SCR 326

  • This case established rules about disclosure as it relates to a fair trial
  • Full disclosure: Crown must share all evidence (even evidence that could help defence and harm their own case) in a timely manner so as to let defence prepare for trial
    • Before this, preliminary inquiries were held so that defence could learn about the Crown's case

Considerations

  1. Do you already have enough disclosure?
  2. Do you want evidence "on the record"?
  3. Will a preliminary inquiry help the defense understand the evidence and witnesses better for trial?
  4. Could a preliminary inquiry help the Crown find and fix weaknesses in their case?
  5. Could the accused face new charges arising out of the evidence?
  6. Can a preliminary inquiry help to settle the case?
  7. Can the client afford both a preliminary inquiry and a trial?

What is sufficient evidence?

  • Crown only needs to establish a prima facie case
    • There must be some evidence on each of the essential elements of the crime
  • Any doubt in the mind of the Provincial Court judge during a preliminary inquiry is resolved in favour of the Crown
How sufficient evidence works

IF the evidence called by the Crown is believed,
COULD the accused be found guilty?

Yes - accused is ordered to stand trial
No - accused is discharged

Preliminary Applications

Exclusion of Witnesses

  • All prospective witnesses are excluded from the courtroom until their testimony is done at trial
    • This is done before the commencement of a trial or preliminary inquiry
    • On the motion of one of the parties or the Court (the judge)

Ban on Publication

  • Evidence taken at a preliminary inquiry shall not be published or broadcast or transmitted in any way until:
    • The accused is discharged or preliminary or
    • The trial in Court of King's Bench has ended
  • Reasons for ban:
    • Reading/watching media coverage may color a witness's testimony
    • Media coverage could influence/bias potential jurors
What if a witness is in the courtroom when they should have been excluded?

They may still testify BUT the judge may place less weight on their evidence

Submissions by Counsel

  • Submissions made by counsel are not evidence

Opening Statement

  • To help the trier of fact understand the evidence to be presented
    • Outlines the facts that they intend to prove
    • Summarizes the theory of their case

Closing Argument

  • To persuade the trier of fact to decide in your favor
    • Emphasizes evidence favorable to their case
    • Emphasizes weaknesses in the other party's case
    • Suggests how the law should be applied to the evidence

Warning

  • The judge may order the accused to stand trial on a lesser included charge or additional charges arising from the evidence
Criminal Code Section 541(2)

Do you wish to say anything in answer to these charges or to any other charges which might have arisen from the evidence led by the prosecution? You are not obliged to say anything, but whatever you do say may be given in evidence against you at your trial. You should not make any confession or admission of guilt because of any promise or threat made to you, but if you do make any statement, it may be given in evidence against you at your trial in spite of the promise or threat.
