Guilty Plea and Sentencing Procedure

Phases of a Criminal Prosecution

  1. Determination of guilt
  2. Determination of punishment

Purposes of Sentencing

  • Denunciation
    • To publicly condemn the unlawful conduct
  • Deterrence
    - General - discourage the general public from committing the same/similar offence in the future
    - Specific - discourage the accused from committing the same/similar offence in the future
    Other purposes:
  • Separate offenders from society if necessary
  • Rehabilitate offenders
  • Provide reparations for harm done
  • Promote a sense of responsibility in offenders to victims and their community

Sentencing Principles

  • A sentence must be proportionate to the gravity of the offence and the degree of responsibility of the offender
  • The judge must attempt to impose a penalty which balances the competing interests of both the guilty party and society
    • Sentence should be increased/reduced to account for relevant aggravating/mitigating circumstances relating to the offence/offender
    • Statutory aggravating factors:
      • Hate crime
      • Domestic violence
      • Victim under 18
      • Abused position of trust/authority
      • Gang/criminal organization
      • Terrorism
  • Similar sentences for similar offenders for similar offences committed in similar circumstances
  • Combined sentence should not be unduly long or harsh
  • All available sanctions other than imprisonment that are reasonable in the circumstances should be considered (particularly indigenous offenders)
  • Sentencing is an individualized process within the framework of the Criminal Code and precedent

Indigenous Offenders

  • A judge/justice should craft a sentence that embraces traditional indigenous sentencing principles where possible
    • The more serious the offence, the less emphasis placed on these considerations
    • R. v. Gladue, [1999] 1 SCR 688
    • R. v. Ipeelee, [2012] 1 SCR 433

Sentencing Procedure

  • Sentencing hearing
    • Can be quick and happen right after verdict
    • OR adjourned to a later time/date
  • After submissions of counsel, there are also be:
    • Order from the judge for a presentence report to help them understand the individual circumstances of the offender/offence
    • Victim impact statements
    • Viva voce evidence
    • Exhibits

Sentencing Options

  • Imprisonment/custodial sentence
    • One of the most severe sentences
    • Sentencing judge is required to use less restrictive sanctions if possible
    • Provincial institution: for sentences < 2 years
    • Federal institution: for sentences > 2 years
      • Imprisonment for life
        • First-degree murder = automatic life - no eligibility for 25 years
        • Second-degree murder = automatic life - no eligibility for 10-25 years (jury may make recommendation for parole ineligibility period)
        • Other offences = no edibility for parole for 7 years
    • Intermittent sentence: term of imprisonment typically served on weekends
    • Conditional sentence (house arrest): option for sentences 2 years or less; served in the community
    • Concurrent sentences: sentences imposed for separate crimes are served at the same time
    • Consecutive sentences: sentences imposed for separate crimes are served one after the other
  • Fine
  • Probation
  • Discharge
  • Dangerous offender
  • Long-term offender

Parole

  • Conditional release of a prisoner before they've served their full custodial sentence
    • To promote successful reintegration into society
  • Remainder of sentence served in the community under supervision
  • When a prisoner is eligible for release, the Parole Board of Canada will assess the risk posed and make a decision on whether to grant parole
    • Temporary release
    • Day parole
    • Full parole
    • Statutory release
  • If parole is breached = fine is imposed

Fine

  • May be imposed for any offence which doesn't have a minimum term of imprisonment
  • If the accused doesn't have the money on hand, they can
    • Ask for time to pay
    • OR work off the fine under the fine option program
  • Deemed days: a prescribed number of days the accused will spend in jail if they don't pay the fine (proportionate to the amount of the fine)

Suspended Sentence & Probation

  • Where an accused pleads guilty or is found guilty of an offence, the judge may choose to suspend the passing of sentence for a period of time
    • Conviction entered
    • Accused is placed on probation for a period of time and required to abide by conditions
    • If probation order is followed successfully, no further punishment
    • If probation order is breached
      • Brought back to court and sentenced on the original charge
      • and/or charged with criminal offence of breach of probation

Probation Order

Statutory Conditions Optional Conditions
Keep the peace and be of good behaviour Report to a probation officer as directed
Appear before the court when required Abstain from consumption of alcohol/drugs
Advise of any change of name, address, employment, or occupation Abstain from use/possession of weapons
Perform community service
Make restitution
Attend treatment program (if willing)
  • Community Service
    • Work done in and for the benefit of the community
    • Done to pay off a monetary fine or as a term of probation
  • Restitution
    • Payment made by the accused to cover actual expenses resulting from the commission of the offence to the victim (broken window, slashed tire...)

Discharge

  • Where an accused pleads guilty or is found guilty of an offence, the judge may grant a discharge instead of entering a conviction
    • Must be in the best interests of the accused and not contrary to public interest
  • 2 types of discharge:
    • Absolute discharge
      • Deemed not to have been convicted
      • No criminal record
    • Conditional discharge
      • Accused is placed on probation for a period of time and required to abide by conditions
      • If probation order is followed successfully, the discharge becomes absolute
      • If probation order is breached
        • Brought back to court and sentenced on the original charge
        • and/or charged with criminal offence of breach of probation
Reminder about criminal records

If accused is given a discharge = no criminal record

If accused is given a suspended sentence = they have a criminal record

Dangerous Offender (DO)

  • Dangerous, violent, and sexual predators who are likely to reoffend in the future
  • Crown can make an application after conviction has been entered, and there would follow a lengthy sentencing hearing with viva voce evidence including psychological evidence
    • If Crown is successful = accused is given an indeterminate term of imprisonment
      • Remain in custody indefinitely
      • Until the risk to reoffend no longer exists

Long-Term Offender (LO)

  • Used where an individual doesn't meet the criteria for a DO designation but has been convicted of a serious personal injury offence and is considered likely to re-offend
  • If declared a LO, the accused will be sentenced to a specified term of incarceration
  • Upon release, accused will be subject to a supervision order in the community for a max of 10 years

Additional Orders

  • Victim fine surcharge
    • Monetary penalty paid by accused in addition to any other sentence
  • DNA order
    • Mandatory for primary designated offences
    • Discretionary for secondary designated offences
  • Firearms and weapons prohibition
    • Sometimes mandatory
  • Sex Offenders Information Registry (SOIRA) order
    • Sometimes mandatory

More About Sentencing

  • Canvassing Section 606(1.1)
    • Accused is entering the guilty plea voluntarily,
    • understands the consequences of pleading guilty, and
    • understands that the Court is not bound by any argument made by counsel
  • Joint submission
    • Joint recommendation for sentence by Crown and defence
    • The judge is not bound to accept a joint submission
  • Statement by accused
    • Accused may address the Court before sentence is imposed
  • If the accused pleads guilty, there is no need for a trial

What happens if the accused...

  • Disputes the facts in a material way?
  • Offers a defence?
  • Doesn't acknowledge the requirements of Section 606(1.1)?
  • Appears to be of unsound mind?
    Then...
  1. Guilty plea is not struck
  2. Not guilty plea is entered on their behalf
  3. Matter is set down for trial in front of a different judge/justice

Lesser Included Offence

  • An offence whose elements are encompassed by a greater offence
  • You can't commit the greater offence without also committing the lesser included offence(s)
Some lesser included offences

Theft + assault = robbery

Assault + possession of a weapon = assault with a weapon

Break and enter + theft = break and enter and commit theft
