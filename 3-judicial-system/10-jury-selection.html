Jury Selection

Charter of Rights and Freedoms

Charter Section 11(f)

  1. Any person charged with an offence has the right...

    (f) to the benefit of trial by jury where the maximum punishment for the offence is imprisonment for five years or a more severe punishment.

  • When it comes to jury trials, the Charter guarantees:
    • Right to have
    • Duty to serve

Advantages of a Jury Trial

  • Verdict must be unanimous
    • If defence can convince just one juror, conviction is barred
  • Jurors can be more subjective and sympathetic
  • Jurors may not fully understand complexities of the law
  • Good defence counsel can influence jurors

Disadvantages of a Jury Trial

  • Jurors can be biased against certain defendants and offences
  • Jurors can be more subjective and sympathetic
  • Jurors may not fully understand complexities of the law
  • Good Crown counsel can influence jurors

Role of the Judge & Jury

  • Jury = judges of the facts
    • Determines what has/hasn't been proven
    • Assesses the credibility of the witnesses and what weight to give their evidence
    • Decides whether the accused is guilty or not guilty
  • Judge = judge of the law
    • Determines what evidence is legally admissible and the jury may hear
    • Instructs the jury on the law and how to apply the law to the facts they find
    • Imposes sentences

Jury Selection

Summons

  • Instructs prospective jurors to attend for jury selection on a specific date
  • Array: pool or prospective jurors, collectively

Juror Eligibility

  • Qualified
    • Canadian citizen
    • Resident of Alberta
    • 18+ years old
  • Excluded
    • Politicians
    • Persons involved in the justice system
    • Persons with criminal charges
  • Exempt
    • Due to:
      • Poor health
      • Personal/professional hardship
      • Recent jury service

Stand By

  • The justice may direct a juror called to stand by for reasons of personal hardship or other reasons

Empanelling a Jury

  • The accused and Crown are entitled to a jury that:
    • Is unbiased
    • Will approach their duties without fear, favour, or prejudice
    • Will decide the case solely on the evidence they hear and see in the courtroom
  • A fully constituted jury:
    • 12 jurors in criminal trials
    • 6 jurors in civil trials
    • 2 alternate jurors selected
      • Can replace a selected juror prior to the start of trial
      • Excused if not needed
      • Generally used in long trials
    • 1 or 2 additional jurors may be selected
      • Serve on jury
      • May deliberate at the end of trial
  • Standard jury selection
    • From a random array
    • Typically not written by a court reporter
  • Challenge for cause jury selection
    • Voir dire
      • Trial within a trial (in-trial hearing)
      • Determines an issue separate from the ultimate issue (guilt or liability)
    • Counsel applies for this if there's concern that the entire array may be biased
    • Discovers whether prospective jurors have preconceived opinions/influenced by media reports about issues in the trial
    • Written by a court reporter and forms part of the transcript if the case is appealed
Transcript notation for a standard jury selection

(JURY SELECTED)

Some questions they may ask in a challenge for jury selection

Have you heard or read about this case before today?

Have you discussed this case with anyone?

Have you formed any opinion of the case?

Do you believe that black men, as a group, are more likely to be violent, generally?

  • Change of venue application
    • A request to have the trial heard in a different judicial district
    • Typically made when there are concerns that the array may be biased
